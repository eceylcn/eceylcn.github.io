I'm fascinated by how people interact with technology, and by what happens in someone's mind while they do. My work spans human-computer interaction, instructional design, and educational technology, and the thread that connects all of it is a real curiosity about how people think, pay attention, and learn while using the systems we put in front of them.

Cognitive science is the lens I reach for most, and lately my focus has been shifting toward the interaction between people and intelligent systems. I don't come from a formal cognitive science background, but it is the space I geek out in, and I think it is exactly where my work becomes most interesting.

What drives the work

Core focus areas.

How much can a mind hold?

When I design learning environments, I care a lot about cognitive load and how to present information so people can hold onto it instead of feeling buried by it.

Who's doing the thinking?

As an AI edtech product manager, I spent a lot of time thinking about cognitive offload: building tools that lift real busywork off teachers and students without quietly doing the thinking they are meant to be doing themselves.

What makes people stay?

When I design games, I study the mechanics that pull players in: the balance between challenge and skill that creates flow, the reward and feedback loops that make progress feel satisfying, and the small moments of anticipation that make people want to come back.

Educational highlights.

Boğaziçi University

Computer Education & Educational Technology

Bachelor of Science · Istanbul, Turkey

I focused on instructional design, building e-learning in Articulate Storyline and courseware in Unity with C#. My capstone was an escape-room science courseware for 5th graders, grounded in constructivist, behaviorist, and cognitivist theory with the ARCS motivation model and Mayer's multimedia principles. I tested it across two schools and ran a full statistical evaluation of the results. Where Is the Toy Treasure (GitHub) →

The product side shaped me most. A Design Thinking for Innovation track pulled me into idea development, project management, and entrepreneurship, where my path toward product management began. Alongside the coursework I taught K-12 coding and robotics, worked with LMS platforms like Canvas, and graduated first in my class with the Dean's Special Award.

Harvard Graduate School of Education

Learning Design, Innovation & Technology

Master of Education · Cambridge, Massachusetts

I was admitted to Harvard Graduate School of Education's LDIT master's program, one of the field's most established programs in learning design and technology and the one most precisely aligned with the work I want to do. Getting in was one of the milestones I'm proudest of, a signal that the research questions I'd been circling for years were worth taking seriously at that level.

After weighing the opportunity honestly, I decided not to enroll. The financial case simply didn't hold, and I wasn't willing to carry a debt I couldn't justify. Instead I chose to continue my graduate studies at Boğaziçi, from Istanbul, pursuing the same questions about learning and technology that drew me to LDIT in the first place.
